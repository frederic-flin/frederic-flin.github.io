Research Topics

Diffusion-limited isothermal metamorphism

Snow, when placed in isothermal conditions and at negative temperature, transforms into a brittle material consisting of small rounded shapes that are typical from microstructures commonly obtained during the sintering of ceramics.

This metamorphism is classically considered as limited by the water vapor diffusion that occurs in the pore space of the snow structure. In order to validate this hypothesis, the water vapor field in small snow samples has been modeled from tri-dimensional images obtained by X-ray microtomography.

Thanks to previously developed algorithms, the curvature field is first estimated at the air-ice interface. By using the Kelvin's equation, one cane define the vapor pressure conditions very close to the interface. Then, the diffusion equation is numerically solved with an iterative method, which provides, after convergence, the water vapor field in the pore space (Fig. 1). The local vapor gradient, which is estimated close to the interface, yields the growth rate in each point of the interface (Fig. 2).

Fig. 1: A 2-D slice of the vapor pressure map computed from a snow image of a sample that has been metamorphosing during 12 days under isothermal conditions. Image edges are about 3 mm wide (300 voxels).

Fig. 2: Map of the local growth rates computed from an image of natural snow assuming a vapor diffusion-limited mechanism in the snow matrix. Image edges are about 3 mm wide (300 voxels). Growth rates range from -0.15 x 10^{-10} m/s (green) to +0.15 x 10^{-10} m/s (red).

The obtained results allow to assess the water vapor diffusion as a possible limiting mechanism for the isothermal metamorphism. However, it does not invalidate other mechanisms that could happen in snow, such as surface reaction phenomena or growth at grain boundaries.

This model, which could be used to simulate the time evolution of small snow structures, consists in a first step toward precise comparisons between different mechanisms.
